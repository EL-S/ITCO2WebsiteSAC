About

In my Computing: Informatics Class, my research Investigation was on how CO2 emissions affect trees growth via environmental and artificial response as a response to the environmental change. My original hypothesis was that as CO2 emissions go up resulting in higher CO2 in the atmosphere, more trees grow via any means possible (artificial plantation, natural growth spurts, etc) as a response to the environmental change. I originally began by gathering data on CO2 emissions for the vast majority of countries by metric tons per capita and other greenhouse gases.

I then proceeded to obtain data relating to tree rings and forested area. This would let me determine how many trees there are, how old the trees are and how big they are. To determine other variables that might affect the growth of plants I also collected data on other topics which I came to a reasonable conclusion would have an effect on the results.

CO2 Emissions by Forested Land Area

I proceeded to graph CO2 Emissions by Forested Land Area. My finding was as follows, as CO2 emissions in a country raise, so to does the Forested Land Area in that country.
It is worth mentioning that although this trend is very clear, other factors might influence this data. For example, sorting by developed countries compared to underdeveloped countries. Furthermore, if the data was further sorted into forested area as a percentage of that country the figures would be less skewed and more representative. It is also important to acknowledge that the population of the country might also have a hidden effect on the result.

CO2 Emissions per Capita and Tree Growth

After graphing more of my secondary factors data, I concluded that there was no real trend in the data globally or locally between CO2 emissions per Capita and Tree Growth before scaling the view area.

It can be seen here that after having removed the results with far more land by restricting the viewing area via scaling due to some values being up to 40 million, the trend is actually the opposite of what my hypothesis predicted. As CO2 emissions go up, the forested area shows a negative trend with more CO2 emissions. Relative to the scale of the data, the trend is so small that it can be considered to have no correlation in reality.

Conclusion

In conclusion, my findings indicate that as Urban Population and CO2 raise, the Forested Land Area in a country subsequently also raise. This strongly seems to suggest that the closer people are living, and the further they are from the forests, and the more CO2 they release, the better the forest growth in the country will be.
